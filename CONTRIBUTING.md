# Contributing to CVSS v5.0 Skull Emoji

Welcome. You have found the most rigorous joke standards development effort currently active on the Internet.

We are working toward a substantially revised submission to the IETF Independent Submissions Editor for 1 April 2027. The 2026 edition was declined on the grounds that CVSS belongs to another organization and the IETF prefers to mock itself. We respect this. The 2027 edition will be self-deprecating commentary on RFC complexity and process, with CVSS as the vehicle rather than the target.

---

## How to contribute

### Open an issue

The primary contribution mechanism is a GitHub issue. Good issues include:

- **Proposed jokes** — new absurdist content for any section
- **Structural suggestions** — ways to reframe the document for the 2027 IETF submission
- **RFC archaeology** — obscure April Fools RFCs we should reference
- **Real-world CVSS observations** — things that are funny because they are true
- **Editorial improvements** — places where the document could be tighter or funnier

Label your issue appropriately:
- `joke-proposal` — a specific new joke or amendment
- `structural` — a suggestion about the document's overall framing
- `rfc-reference` — a normative or informative reference we should add
- `editorial` — a prose improvement

### Submit a PR

If you have specific text to propose, fork the repo and open a PR against `main`. Please:

- Preserve RFC plain text formatting (72-character line width)
- Include a brief description of why your change is funnier than what it replaces
- Reference any relevant issues

### Join a working call

We hold monthly editorial working calls. Open an issue tagged `working-call` to get the invite, or email [trey@propertools.be](mailto:trey@propertools.be).

Calls are conducted in accordance with RFC 1925 Truth #11: *"Every old idea will be proposed again with a different name and a different presentation, regardless of whether it worked the first time."*

## Leaderboard

Outstanding contributions are recognized in [LEADERBOARD.md](./LEADERBOARD.md). Selection criteria:

- **Laughed out loud** — the editorial team laughed audibly upon reading
- **Technically accurate** — the joke is funnier because it is also correct
- **RFC-compliant** — proper use of MUST, SHOULD, MAY, and the Evil Bit
- **Recursively Funny** — always hilarious, special category
- **Survives peer review** — at least two reviewers must be able to explain why it is funny in at least two different ways

---

## Conduct

This is a technical comedy collaboration. Be funny. Be kind. Don't be the person who explains the joke. If you have to explain it, it wasn't funny.

Reading the room is the heart of effective comedy.

## A note on the 2027 strategy

The IETF Submission Editor's rejection was specific and solvable. The reframe we are working toward:

The document should read as affectionate self-mockery of IETF process, RFC archaeology, and the peculiar culture of standards development — with CVSS as a familiar, fondly-regarded example of what happens when you apply rigorous process to inherently fuzzy problems. The skull emoji scoring system becomes a commentary on whether numerical precision in vulnerability scoring was ever really the point, and also how quickly we learn to dance to the most easily countable metrics.

This is a _goldmine of material_ for jokes. It is also kinder. IETF has shown throughout its history that it can take a joke, at times by actually being one.

Contributions that advance this reframe constructively are particularly welcome.
